The problem

Every family means to write it down. Almost none do.

You mean to sit her down with a recorder. You mean to ask your dad how he met your mom, about the war, about the house he grew up in. Then a birthday passes, then a year — and one day the stories are gone, and there's no one left to ask. Heirloom is the one who finally asks. Every week. Patiently. Before it's too late.

How it works

Three steps. Your parent only does one of them.

1

A weekly call

At a time you choose, we phone your parent for about 25 minutes. A warm interviewer named June asks about their life — and, the secret, actually listens, remembering every name and story from one week to the next.

2

Every word, kept

Each conversation is recorded and written down. Nothing rushed, nothing lost. Your parent controls every topic — anything they'd rather not discuss, they simply leave be, and June moves on.

3

A keepsake, for good

Afterward, their stories become a book in their own words, plus a short recording of favorite moments in their own voice. Yours to print, share, and read aloud for generations.

Let's be honest

Yes — the interviewer is an artificial intelligence. Here's why that's the gift.

A human interviewer gets tired, forgets, cancels, and would cost a small fortune for a year of weekly hours. June never does. She has infinite patience for a long story, never interrupts, never checks the clock, and calls every single week without fail. She remembers the name of every cousin and the make of every car your mother mentions — and brings them up later, so your mother feels genuinely heard. And a real person from Heirloom listens to every call and writes the book by hand. The care is human. The patience is inexhaustible.

What your family keeps

Two things a photograph can't hold.

The book

Their words, in chapters

A book of their life told in their own phrasing — not smoothed into generic prose. If they say "we was," the book says "we was." The kind of thing read aloud at anniversaries, and one day, at a funeral.

The voice

Five minutes of them

A short recording cut from the calls — their actual laugh, their actual way of telling it. The page a family frames; the file a grandchild plays long after.

Questions families ask

The honest answers.

What does my parent have to do? +

Answer the phone. That's the whole job. No apps, no computer, no setup — we handle everything. They just talk.

Isn't it strange to be interviewed by an AI? +

Most people forget within a minute or two — June is warm and genuinely curious, and she's honest about what she is if anyone asks. What they remember is the feeling of being truly listened to.

What about their privacy? +

Both you and your parent sign a simple one-page consent. Calls are recorded only to make your keepsakes, stored privately, and deleted whenever you ask. See our promises above.

What if they're hard of hearing? +

June speaks slowly and clearly, repeats and rephrases gladly, and never rushes or talks over a pause. The calls are built for exactly this.

Can they refuse a question? +

Always. "Let's leave that one" is a complete answer, and June moves on warmly, without making it awkward. The stories are theirs to give.

The reader's slip

Now tell me what's wrong with it.

Galleys don't go out for praise — they go out to be marked up. Three questions. Answer one, answer all three, or just reply with the part where you winced. Reference them by number if it's easier.

  1. i.

    When you hit the word "AI," what happened in your gut?

    Be precise about the feeling, not the argument. Did it land as patient and reliable — or as a robot phoning your mother? This one question is the whole reason you're reading this.

  2. ii.

    Would you tell your own mother it's an AI?

    If your instinct was "I'd do this, but I just wouldn't mention that part" — please say so plainly. It's the single most useful thing you could tell me, and the hardest for me to hear.

  3. iii.

    If it's a no, is it the price — or is it the premise?

    "Too expensive," "wrong time," "my dad wouldn't pick up" are all fixable. "It feels wrong to let a machine do this" is not. I need to know which one you actually mean.
